# Satisfaction Loop: Power Grid Operator

**Loop satisfaction:** 0.8

**A. Rhythm — strong**
Read demand → solve equation → allocate power → watch city react → next surge. Four beats with clear transitions. The equation is the thinking beat. The allocation is the decision beat. The city reacting is the payoff beat. The next surge is the tension beat.

**B. Action consequence — strong**
Every allocation has immediate visible consequence: district lights up or dims. Overload creates a flash. Blackout triggers sirens. The grid map is a live feedback surface — the player sees the result of every action in real time.

**C. Feedback quality — strong**
Equation solved → score increment. Power routed → district glows. Blackout → sirens + map dims + citizen radio. Storm → source goes red + new equation. Multiple feedback channels operating simultaneously.

**D. Depth through repetition — strong**
Round 1: one district, simple missing addend (5 + x = 12). Round 10: two districts competing, two-step equation (2x + 3 = 15), one source failing. The player discovers that solving faster matters because time saved on one district buys time for another. Strategy emerges from repetition.

**E. Reward shape — strong**
Zero-blackout shift > some-blackout shift > barely-survived shift. Time bonus rewards speed. Efficiency bonus rewards optimal allocation. Multiple success tiers exist naturally without needing artificial star ratings.

**F. Friction source — correct skill**
The friction comes from: (1) algebraic computation speed, (2) allocation prioritization under scarcity. Both are intended skills. The friction does NOT come from: unclear UI, hidden information, or motor difficulty. The grid map should make all information glanceable.

**Strongest source of satisfaction:**
The allocation triage — deciding which district gets power when you can't power all of them — creates a "tough choice, right call" feeling that pure equation-solving cannot.

**Strongest source of flatness:**
If early levels have only one district and one source, the loop is just "solve equation, route power, done." No allocation decision = no strategic satisfaction. The allocation layer must appear by L3.

**Main repetition risk:**
If demand patterns are fixed per level (same districts, same demand values), the second play of the same level is identical. Demand must be randomized to create genuine replay variation.

**Recommendation before build:**
Ensure that by L3, the player faces a real allocation decision (2 districts, not enough power for both). This is the moment the game stops being a quiz and starts being a game. If this moment doesn't work, the concept doesn't work.
